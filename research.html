Research

Overview

My research examines how personality traits, temperament, and socio-contextual factors shape individual development from childhood through early adulthood. A central focus is understanding the pathways through which dispositional characteristics influence educational outcomes among Mexican-origin youth, drawing on long-term longitudinal data.

I am particularly interested in questions that sit at the intersection of personality science, developmental psychology, and the sociology of education — asking not only whether personality predicts school success, but how (via intrapersonal and interpersonal mechanisms) and for whom (via family, school, neighborhood, and cultural moderators).

Research Lines

Personality & Temperament as Predictors of School Outcomes

Using data from the Coping with Immigration Study — an 18-year longitudinal study of Mexican-origin families — I investigate how Big Five personality traits and temperament trajectories across late childhood and adolescence predict academic achievement, school engagement, and attainment. This line of work also examines the incremental validity of temperament over broad Big Five domains, and tests intrapersonal (e.g., self-efficacy, motivation) and interpersonal (e.g., teacher relationships, peer dynamics) mediators of these effects.

Psychiatric Problems, Mental Health, & Academic Achievement

A parallel line of work examines whether internalizing and externalizing psychiatric problems — beyond temperament — prospectively predict poor school achievement. This helps disentangle the unique contributions of normative personality variation and clinical-level problems to educational disparities.

Lifespan Development of Personality & Well-Being

In collaboration with colleagues, I study the stability, change, and co-development of Big Five traits and well-being across the lifespan, including cohort differences between Mexican-origin youth and their parents. This work speaks to foundational questions about when and why personality changes across development.

Growth Mindset: Antecedents & Interventions

I examine the developmental antecedents of growth mindset in adolescence and early adulthood, and the mechanisms through which social and emotional learning (SEL) interventions promote adaptive motivational beliefs. This includes a large randomized-controlled trial of Indonesian youth.

Class Identity & Materialistic Values

This work investigates trajectories of materialistic values across adolescence and their prospective associations with life satisfaction in early adulthood, as well as the roles of familism, socioeconomic factors, and parent characteristics in shaping class identity among Mexican-origin youth.

Longitudinal Data

Much of my empirical work draws on the Coping with Immigration Study (CWI), a 14–18 year longitudinal study of Mexican-origin families in Northern California, led by Richard W. Robins and colleagues at UC Davis.
